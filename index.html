Manifesto

Latin is taught in about 3% of English state schools. That is not because the other 97% couldn't do it. It is because the way Latin has always been taught assumes a particular kind of pupil — in a particular kind of school, with a particular kind of background.

I teach Latin in Brixton. 27.6% of my pupils achieve grade 5 in English and Maths at GCSE. 55.7% speak English as an additional language. Latin is compulsory throughout KS3. My pupils are doing it — and the ones who struggle everywhere else are sometimes the ones who flourish here most.

The subject is not the problem. The pedagogy is.

EdTech

Labyrinth

A mastery-based Latin learning platform — built from scratch, deployed in two London state schools.

Currently live at Trinity Academy, Brixton and the West London Free School — the Sunday Times UK Comprehensive School of the Year 2026. Spaced-repetition flashcards with Easy/Hard sorting. Timed multiple-choice questions. A written translation mode that accepts the full range of valid renderings for any Latin sentence — handling typos, alternative word orders, and genuine linguistic variation.

The teacher portal shows live analytics: a leaderboard, time spent per exercise, and a breakdown of the most commonly missed verb forms across the cohort.

The hardest problem was automating translation marking. Any Latin sentence has dozens of valid English renderings. No existing platform had solved this. I worked iteratively with AI to curate valid translations for each sentence and built the marking scheme from that output.

Currently in discussion for expansion within Future Academies and across its eleven schools.

Seed

The vocabulary architecture problem

Latin vocabulary is almost universally taught in dictionary order — alphabetically, or by the sequence they happen to appear in a textbook. No second language acquisition research supports this. Thomas Tinkham's foundational work demonstrated that thematic vocabulary clusters produce measurably better retention than semantic-field grouping.

The Dickinson College Commentaries core list of 1,000 high-frequency Latin lemmas represents the field's best frequency data — yet it is typically presented alphabetically in the classroom.

The question I keep returning to: if we organised Latin vocabulary the way acquisition research actually recommends — by frequency, by thematic scenario, by semantic network — what would that look like? And who would it serve that the current system doesn't? Labyrinth is my attempt at an answer.

Seed

A second brain for a second language

The analogy I keep reaching for: a student's encounters with Latin vocabulary should accumulate into something — a connected map, a personal knowledge graph, a second brain for a second language. Not a list to be memorised and forgotten.

The words of a language are a network, not a sequence. Latin, of all languages, makes this visible: the word amor radiates outward to amare, to amicus, to inimicus, to amabilis, to amans. Those connections are not incidental — they are the language. Teaching them as isolated items is not just inefficient. It is a category error.

Most impressive things

I solo excavated the first coin hoard ever found at an ancient Roman town in Italy. I've done three summers as a trench supervisor at the site and feature in a BBC article about it. While doing fieldwork with a team of archaeologists, I'd been assigned to what the team thought was a disused well, as punishment for digging too aggressively the day before. I built Ancient Troy from scratch in Minecraft over 60 hours, street by street, using Schliemann's 19th-century archaeological maps, then reimagined it as a cyberpunk city in SketchUp and rendered it as a 360° VR experience. What's more, my experimental photography made during the process was showcased in the Royal Academy's Young Artists show, was a Sovereign Art Prize finalist, and a print sold at Phillips Auction House.

Most risk taken

Same meal, twice a day, six weeks. A 48-hour food and water fast, shivering on a bike in three jumpers before the final weigh-in. 5.6% body fat. Strongest-ever candidate. Cambridge half marathon in 1:36 right after.

The biggest risk was financial: £400 upfront, no profit for success. At the time it was everything in my account. I did it purely for the challenge.
